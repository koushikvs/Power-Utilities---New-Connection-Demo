Chapter 4 · Scrutiny

The questions that will be asked in the room.

Every serious AI program in a regulated utility gets stress-tested before it gets funded. This page addresses what a CIO, VP Engineering, regulatory affairs lead, or CFO is likely to raise — answered plainly, with the assumptions visible.

A note on scope: This program separates two value propositions with different risk profiles. (1) Coordination and workflow automation — eliminating queue time, document chasing, and handoffs. Deployable today with low regulatory friction; the business case stands on this alone. (2) Autonomous engineering design generation — valuable, but requires a formal validation program and regulatory engagement before production use. The recommended roadmap treats these as sequential phases, not a single launch.
A note on numbers: This document intentionally avoids committing to specific dollar projections, headcount reduction figures, or payback periods ahead of Phase 1 pilot validation against your utility's actual operating data. Ranges, mechanisms, and structural claims are provided; absolute dollars are not. The cost build-up below shows the shape of the savings — which levers contribute, in what relative proportion — so the methodology is transparent without pre-committing to numbers that would need to be defended in a rate case or board meeting.
1
Regulatory & PE Liability
Who bears PE liability for an AI-generated design?
The stamping Professional Engineer — same as today, with no change to the liability model. The Design-from-Zero Agent produces a draft package, not a final design. The PE conducts an independent structured review, may request revisions (the residential simulation shows this explicitly), and issues a formal electronic stamp with a written attestation. State licensing boards require PEs to personally review what they seal — this program does not alter that obligation. What changes is the quality of the starting draft: PE prep time drops from approximately 8 hours to 2–3 hours because the agent has already performed load calculations, route optimisation, and BOM assembly. The PE's engineering judgement and professional liability remain fully intact.
Key framing for engineering teams: The agent is a drafting assistant. The PE is the design authority. This distinction should be documented in the utility's AI governance policy before any design-generation capability goes live.
Does deploying this program require PUC or state regulatory approval?
It depends on which layer is being deployed. The coordination and workflow automation layer — intake, qualification, contract routing, payment orchestration, dispatch — operates within existing tariff authority and does not require new regulatory filings in most US jurisdictions. It is functionally equivalent to improving an internal business process. The design generation layer is a different matter: using an AI model to produce designs that are then PE-reviewed and used to issue construction orders warrants proactive engagement with the state PUC and the state engineering licensing board. The recommended approach is to file a voluntary compliance inquiry before Phase 2 begins, rather than discover a compliance issue after deployment.
Do not deploy design generation in production without a regulatory engagement plan. The coordination layer can go live independently and should.
How does a 1 MW commercial connection interact with FERC and state DSIS requirements?
Loads at or above the state PUC threshold — typically 500 kW to 1 MW depending on jurisdiction — trigger a Distribution System Impact Study, a regulatory process that runs on its own timeline, typically 45–55 business days. No agent can compress this timeline. It is a regulatory constraint, not an operational one. What the agent does is initiate the DSIS on Day 1, in parallel with design, contracting, and construction work, rather than waiting until after design approval — a sequencing change that recovers 8–12 days that legacy processes lost to sequential queuing. The commercial scenario in the simulation reflects this accurately: the DSIS runs as a parallel track, not a blocker, and the agent flags energisation as contingent on DSIS clearance throughout the workflow.
Does NERC CIP or state cybersecurity regulation affect the agent's access to operational data?
The agents read and write to CCM, SAP ISU, WAM, GIS, and SFS through existing API layers — they do not have direct access to SCADA, EMS, or any bulk electric system control systems. GIS access is limited to the distribution network model (not transmission). This architecture keeps the agent environment outside the NERC CIP Electronic Security Perimeter. That said, the utility's IT security team should review the API access scope and confirm that the agent service accounts are provisioned under the principle of least privilege. An OT/IT boundary review is standard practice before any new system integration and should be included in the Phase 1 deployment checklist.
How does this affect our next rate case? Does the OpEx reduction eventually flow back to ratepayers?
Yes — and this needs to be planned for, not discovered. New-connection coordination OpEx sits in O&M, which is a pass-through in the traditional regulated-utility model. In a rate case 2–4 years after deployment, the PUC will look at actual O&M and may rebase allowances downward, reducing the utility's retained benefit. Three strategic responses a CFO should consider: (1) Front-load the benefit window — most of the savings accrue in Years 1–3 before the next rate case; model the value on that basis, not in perpetuity. (2) Redirect engineering capacity rather than reduce it — engineers redeployed to grid-modernisation studies generate rate-base investment that earns allowed ROE, converting an OpEx saving into a regulated asset-base growth opportunity. This is the single most important financial lever in the program. Target redeployment scale is sized post-pilot based on actual time-saved-per-case data; it is deliberately not pre-committed here. (3) Capitalise the AI platform where permissible — work with regulatory counsel to determine whether implementation spend qualifies as a capital investment under the jurisdiction's software accounting treatment. The business case should be built with and without rate-case rebasing assumptions; showing both gives the board a defensible view.
This is why the engineering-capacity redirect matters more than the direct OpEx saving. The OpEx number attracts regulatory attention; the rate-base growth opportunity from accelerated grid-mod does not.
2
Data & Model Trust
What is the design model trained on, and how was it validated?
The model is trained on the utility's own historical as-built design packages — typically 5–10 years of stamped residential and light commercial designs, sourced from the utility's GIS and document management system. It is not trained on generic internet data or designs from other utilities. Validation uses a holdout set of historical designs, measuring agreement with the PE's final stamped output across load calculations, conductor sizing, route selection, and BOM line items. Initial go-live requires ≥95% agreement on residential cases over a 500-design validation set. Results are documented and made available for regulatory inspection.
The most significant deployment risk is training data quality. Utilities with incomplete or inconsistently digitised historical design records will need a data remediation workstream before model training can begin. This should be assessed in the pre-Phase 2 scoping.
How is model drift detected and managed over time?
Every PE-stamped design becomes a labelled training record. PE revisions — where the PE modifies the agent's draft before stamping — are flagged as correction signals and reviewed at 90-day intervals. A drift monitoring dashboard tracks revision frequency: if the rate of PE corrections rises above a defined threshold (default: 8% of designs), the model is flagged for retraining before it materially increases PE workload. Annual model retraining is conducted using the prior 12 months of stamped designs. This cadence is documented in the AI governance policy as a standing operational commitment, not an ad-hoc activity.
What happens when GIS or WAM data is stale or incorrect?
The agent includes an explicit data-freshness check at the premise reconciliation step. When a GIS asset record has not been field-verified within a configurable threshold (default: 12 months), the agent triggers an automatic WAM re-sync before proceeding with design generation. This is shown in the residential simulation: a stale transformer record is detected, the WAM re-sync completes in 38 minutes with no human intervention required, and design generation proceeds on confirmed data. The agent never silently accepts stale data. If re-sync fails or produces an irreconcilable conflict, the case is escalated to a field verification queue and held until the data is confirmed.
This is a meaningful improvement over the legacy process, where stale GIS data was typically discovered during field execution — an expensive point in the workflow to find a data error.
The simulation shows a "completeness score." Is that the approval gate?
No — explicitly not. The completeness score is an internal diagnostic that checks whether the design package contains all 22 required elements: load calculation, conductor sizing, transformer headroom check, BOM completeness, route clearances, applicable code references, and so on. A high score means the PE is starting from a well-formed draft. A low score means the PE should scrutinise further and the case may need senior engineer review. Every design, regardless of score, is routed to PE review. The score is visible to the PE as context — not as a recommendation to approve or a proxy for engineering judgement. This distinction is documented in the PE review protocol and should be part of the PE training programme at go-live.
3
IT Integration & Risk
Does this require replacing SAP ISU, GIS, or any system of record?
No. The architecture is an orchestration layer over existing systems — CCM, SAP ISU, SAP WAM, GIS, SAP SFS, and DocuSign — via their published API interfaces. No system of record is replaced, migrated, or re-implemented. This is a deliberate design principle: stabilise and extend what exists rather than introduce replacement risk. The integration risk profile is API availability and data quality, not system transformation. Utilities currently on SAP S/4HANA can use the same integration layer; the agent does not require ISU-specific APIs where S/4HANA equivalents exist.
What happens when an API call fails or a downstream system is unavailable?
Each agent implements a circuit-breaker pattern. On a transient failure, the agent retries up to three times with exponential backoff. If retries are exhausted, the case is escalated to a human work queue with the specific failure reason and full case context. No transaction is silently dropped. The agent preserves all data collected to that point in a persistent state store, so when the system recovers, the workflow resumes from the last confirmed step — it does not restart from the beginning. All failure events are logged in a tamper-evident audit trail.
The reliability of this pattern depends on the stability of the utility's existing middleware and API layer. Utilities with older SOAP-based SAP integrations should assess middleware maturity as part of the Phase 1 integration design. This is a discovery item, not a blocker.
How is customer and operational data protected?
Customer PII is handled within the utility's existing CCM data perimeter — the agent reads and writes through CCM APIs and does not create a separate customer data store. Engineering design data (GIS coordinates, asset IDs, load profiles) is treated as operational data and is not transmitted to external AI training infrastructure. Model training runs on-premises or in the utility's private cloud environment using de-identified historical design packages. All agent-to-system interactions are logged in a tamper-evident audit trail. Data retention policies for agent logs follow the utility's existing records management schedule.
If the Dispatch Agent sends a crew and an incident occurs in the field, who owns the liability?
Operational liability does not transfer to the agent. Final crew assignment authority rests with the dispatch supervisor — the agent produces a recommended plan which the supervisor reviews and releases. The SFS audit record captures the supervisor's release action against each assignment. In the event of a field incident, the standard incident investigation (OSHA-reportable or otherwise) examines the chain: crew action, supervisor release decision, and the inputs that informed the agent's recommendation. The agent's input set — crew qualifications, rest-period compliance, weather, skills match, site risk flags — is logged and available for incident review, which is a net improvement over current-state dispatch where reasoning is often undocumented. The utility's safety insurer and legal team should be engaged in Phase 1 to formalise the supervisor-authority model and update the dispatch standard operating procedure. Union consultation on this topic is required where collective-bargaining agreements govern dispatch practices.
The liability model is "agent recommends, human authorises" — the same structure used for flight dispatch advisory systems and hospital clinical decision support. It is legally well-understood; what changes is how the reasoning is documented.
What happens during storm response? Do the agents interfere with outage restoration?
The program includes an explicit storm-mode degradation path. When the utility's incident management system declares a storm event or a major restoration operation, the Dispatch Agent automatically yields SFS capacity to storm restoration — new-connection dispatch is queued and flagged to customers with a transparent status update ("scheduling paused due to storm response; your energisation date is being re-evaluated"). Intake, qualification, design, and contracting continue to run so the paperwork pipeline stays full, but no new construction orders are released to the field until storm-mode is cleared. Customer-facing messaging is drafted by the Intake Agent and released by the communications lead — not auto-published — so external tone is human-controlled during emergencies. The degraded-mode handoff and resumption behaviour should be validated in a Phase 1 tabletop exercise with the emergency operations team before production go-live.
Storm-mode behaviour is a common regulatory examination topic. Documenting it explicitly in the operations manual is both good practice and sound risk management.
4
Labor & Change Management
Does this program eliminate PE or engineering jobs?
No — it eliminates PE prep time and administrative queue management, not PE positions. In most utilities, engineers spend a significant share of their time on document assembly, queue coordination, and rework chasing rather than engineering judgement. This program redirects that capacity toward higher-value work: grid modernisation studies, interconnection backlog review, renewable integration design, and complex commercial cases. Engineering team size stays flat or grows as the grid-mod programme accelerates. The headcount model targets redeployment, not reduction. The scale of redeployable capacity — how many engineers, across which specialties — is intentionally not pre-committed here; it is a Phase 1 output, sized against actual time-saved-per-case data from the pilot, not a benchmark assumption.
The strongest framing for engineering teams: "We are giving you back the engineering work. The agents handle the paperwork."
What happens to the CSRs who currently handle new-connection intake and coordination?
Straight-through residential cases — estimated at roughly 58% of volume — are handled end-to-end without CSR involvement. The remaining ~42% still require skilled CSR support: complex commercial cases, escalations, customers who prefer telephone interaction, and accommodation requests. The target operating model moves CSR roles from data-entry and queue management toward case management and customer advocacy for complex interactions. In the base-case design, natural attrition over a 24-month transition window absorbs most of the capacity reduction, with no involuntary reductions projected. The workforce transition plan — including any specific redeployment or training pathways — should be sized against the utility's actual volume, current staffing model, and bargaining agreements in the Phase 1 planning workstream, not pre-committed to a benchmark number here.
How are field crews affected, and does this require union consultation?
Field crews see two changes: optimised dispatch scheduling (how they receive assignments) and AI-assisted as-built photo validation (the closeout step). Both are delivered through the existing SFS mobile interface — no new device or app is required. Dispatch optimisation benefits crews directly by reducing same-day schedule changes and improving parts staging accuracy. The as-built validation step adds a structured photo-capture workflow but removes the manual paper-based closeout form; net field time impact is negligible. Union consultation on the dispatch scheduling algorithm — specifically the parameters governing crew assignment and rest-period compliance — is recommended as part of deployment planning and should be initiated in Phase 1.

What about payback period and total annual savings?
These are deliberately not quoted in this document. Payback period is a function of (a) implementation cost envelope, which is driven by API integration complexity and data readiness, (b) per-connection savings, which is utility-specific, and (c) connection volume. All three inputs need to be established against the utility's actual operating profile before a payback figure becomes meaningful. Directionally: for a mid-sized US utility with modern API infrastructure and clean GIS data, payback is measured in months rather than years on direct OpEx savings alone, before any engineering-capacity redirect value is included. A firmer range will come out of the Phase 1 scoping exercise, with pilot data confirming or adjusting it in Phase 2.
Committing to a specific payback period ahead of pilot validation creates a hostage to fortune — both internally (versus actuals) and externally (in future rate cases). The discipline of holding back the number until it can be grounded in your data is a feature, not a gap.
What does the implementation roadmap look like, and what are the go/no-go gates?
A three-phase approach separates low-risk coordination automation from higher-risk design generation, with explicit validation gates before each phase advances.
Phase 1
Months 1–6
Low risk
Coordination layer only — Intake, Qualification, Contract, Payment, Dispatch
Deploy agent coordination on a single service territory pilot, targeting 300 residential connections. No design generation. Estimated cycle time improvement: 25–30%. Establishes baseline metrics, builds operator confidence, and surfaces API integration issues before higher-stakes capabilities go live. Go/no-go gate: ≥25% cycle time reduction, <0.5% transaction failure rate, field rework rate unchanged.
Phase 2
Months 7–15
Validation required
Design generation pilot — advisory mode with parallel PE review
Deploy Design-from-Zero in shadow mode: agent generates draft, PE independently reviews from scratch, outputs compared. Validate agreement rate ≥95% over 500 cases across residential and light commercial — this is the internal engineering QA gate, not a regulatory clearance. In parallel, a separate 12–18 month regulatory engagement track runs with the state PUC and engineering licensing board (TBPE in Texas, BPELSG in California, and equivalents): pre-filing notice, validation study review, public comment period, and formal no-objection. State boards have publicly signalled caution on AI-assisted design, and 500 cases is not by itself sufficient to clear them. No construction orders are released from agent-drafted designs until both tracks complete. Go/no-go gate: ≥95% PE agreement rate, regulatory no-objection received, PE training programme complete.
Phase 3
Months 16–22
Full deployment
Full orchestrated workflow — all agents, all connection types
Full agent-orchestrated workflow including design generation, with PE reviewing the agent draft rather than generating from scratch. Target cycle time improvement: 55–60% for residential, 40–45% for commercial (DSIS-constrained). Ongoing: quarterly drift monitoring, annual model retraining, annual regulatory reporting. Complex commercial cases (>2 MW) remain on manual design track pending extended validation.
What does Phase 1 pilot cost, and what does success look like?
Phase 1 cost is deliberately not quoted as a firm figure here — it is driven primarily by the condition of the utility's existing API layer, the availability of CCM and SAP ISU REST interfaces, and the quality of GIS and WAM data. A utility with modern REST APIs and clean master data will sit at the low end of the range; one requiring middleware development or a master-data remediation workstream will sit materially higher. The cost envelope should be established in a scoping exercise ahead of Phase 1 kickoff, with a firm budget committed only after the integration discovery is complete. Success at the end of Phase 1 is defined by outcomes, not dollars: (1) cycle time reduced by ≥25% on the pilot cohort versus the prior-year control group in the same territory; (2) human touchpoints per connection meaningfully reduced (the full target comes with design generation in Phase 2); (3) transaction failure rate <0.5%; (4) field rework rate unchanged or improved; (5) operator satisfaction score ≥7/10 from CSR and dispatch teams. The pilot should be run with a dedicated operations monitor who documents every edge case and failure mode — these learnings directly shape Phase 2 design and are more valuable than the KPI results alone.
