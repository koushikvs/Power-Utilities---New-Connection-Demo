Chapter 1 · A day in the reimagined P&U

From 47 days
to 19 days.

A new connection at a mid-sized US utility used to mean 10 manual steps, 6 systems, 4 handoffs, and 47 days of average cycle time. Today, builder Alex Martinez requests a 400-amp service — and an autonomous agent orchestra eliminates every day of queue time, stale-data delays, and coordination handoffs. The crews still dig the trench. The PE still stamps the design. The agents compress everything in between.

The program does not reduce field-execution time. Linemen, trenching, conductor pull, and meter set are paced by crew availability and physical work. The 28 days recovered are entirely from queue time, handoff latency, and document preparation — i.e., work that adds no customer value today.

Dollar projections are intentionally absent from this document. Per-connection savings, annual OpEx impact, payback period, and implementation envelope are all utility-specific; they should be modelled against your actual labour rates, rework rate, connection volume, and integration complexity during the Phase 1 scoping exercise. The FAQ cost build-up shows the structure of the savings (which mechanisms contribute, in what proportion) without committing to absolute numbers. Engineering-capacity redirect figures are sized post-pilot — they depend on volume, case mix, and the residential/commercial split at your utility. "PE agreement rate" is the internal engineering QA gate; separate state PUC and licensing-board engagement runs on a parallel 12–18 month track — see FAQ.
